Past Research

Senior Independent Study

A year-long research and writing project which culminated in an 85 page paper entitled, “Collective Memories of the American Civil War in Kentucky Historical Romances, 1890-1915.” This project examines how Kentucky bestselling authors at the turn of the twentieth century looked back upon the situation of Kentucky during the Civil War and how their historical fiction reflected popular collective memories of the conflict around 1900.

Junior Independent Study in History

A semester-long research project completed at the College of Wooster which culminated in a 30 page thesis, "Western Equal or Yellow Peril: Varying American Perceptions of Japanese Imperial Policy in Formosa and Korea, 1893-1915." This project examines the ways in which Japanese imperialism was portrayed in American media at the turn of the twentieth century. It received the James R. Turner Prize in History, awarded to the most distinguished Junior Independent Study Thesis.

Junior Independent Study in English

A semester-long research project completed at the Newberry Library in Chicago which culminated in a 40 page thesis, "Imagined Savagery: British Perceptions of Native Americans in Late-Victorian Adventure Fiction." This project explores the representations of Native American characters in the late-nineteenth-century British juvenile adventure fiction of R.M. Ballantyne, G.A. Henty, and W.H.G. Kingston.

Experience

Intern at the National Museum of American History

During the summer of 2016, I worked as an intern under curator Debbie Schaefer-Jacobs in the Department of Home and Community Life at the Smithsonian's National Museum of American History. There, I aided in the preparation of a group of web labels for the online publication of a group of Civil War lithographs from Harry T. Peters "America on Stone" Collection. Read a blog I published while at the museum here.

Semester in the Humanities at the Newberry Library

During the fall of 2015, I enrolled in a semester-long seminar at the Newberry Library in Chicago, dedicated to the theme, “Knowing Your Place: Human and Social Geography.” During this time, I completed a 40-page research project in fulfillment of the Junior Independent Study Thesis in English for the College of Wooster. I also gained experience working as an assistant in the Digital Imaging Services Department.

Wooster Digital History Project

During the summer of 2014, I particpated in the Wooster Digital History Project, contributing to an Omeka site dedicated to the history of the city of Wooster, Ohio. I created exhibits on Wooster during the Civil War and the temperance movement, and created an interactive map of Wooster Cemetery.
